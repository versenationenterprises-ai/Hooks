Every hook is a framework. Not a script.

For every video you film, pick one from each category — then make sure all four match in energy. A heavy topic needs heavy audio. A curiosity visual needs a curiosity text overlay. That alignment is what makes people stop. The mismatch is why they don't.

"Lock the hook before you film anything else. The first 5 seconds aren't the intro — they're the entire argument for why someone should stay."

Layer 01

Verbal

What you say in the first 5 seconds. The spoken pattern interrupt that earns the next 10.

Layer 02

Visual

What the viewer sees before you open your mouth. Movement, angle, entry — the eye stops before the brain does.

Layer 03

Audio

The sound layer that makes everything land harder. Most creators ignore this. That's the gap.

Layer 04

Text

The overlay that reinforces, adds tension, or creates a second hook running parallel to what you're saying.

The rule: All four layers must be congruent in energy. Heavy topic = heavy audio + dark visual + tension text. Miss one and you lose them.

01 — Verbal Hooks

What you say in the first 5 seconds.

The spoken pattern interrupt. This is the only hook most creators think about — which means getting all four right is already a competitive advantage.

V — 01

The Countdown Promise

Framework

In [timeframe], I'm going to show you exactly how to [specific outcome] — and you won't need [common objection].

Example

"In 60 seconds I'm going to show you exactly how to get your first DM from a stranger — and you won't need to run a single ad."

Why it works

The timeframe creates urgency. The objection removal kills the skip instinct before it fires.

V — 02

The Confession Opener

Framework

I used to [embarrassing belief or action] — until I realized [paradigm shift].

Example

"I used to post every single day for 4 months straight — until I realized I was building an audience that would never buy anything."

Why it works

Confession = instant relatability. The shift earns authority without a credential drop.

V — 03

The Specific Number Drop

Framework

I [did specific action] for [exact timeframe] and got [exact result] — here's the one thing that changed everything.

Example

"I posted 37 videos in 90 days and got 11 paying clients — here's the one thing that changed everything."

Why it works

Odd numbers feel real. Round numbers feel made up. Always use specific, odd numbers.

V — 04

The Mid-Thought Entry

Framework

— and that's the part nobody talks about. [Continue the fake mid-conversation topic immediately.]

Example

"— and that's the part nobody talks about. Your content isn't failing because it's bad. It's failing because it's invisible."

Why it works

The dash signals you dropped in mid-scene. No intro. No greeting. Pure pattern interrupt.

V — 05

The Contrarian Cold Open

Framework

Stop [widely accepted advice]. It's actually making your [problem] worse.

Example

"Stop posting more content. It's actually making your account grow slower."

Why it works

Disagreement in the first word creates cognitive dissonance. They have to stay to find out why.

V — 06

The Direct Address Warning

Framework

If you're [specific situation], do NOT [common action] until you watch this.

Example

"If you're a business owner posting content and it's not bringing you leads, do NOT hire a content team until you watch this."

Why it works

Specificity in the "if" filters the right viewer — and makes everyone else feel like they're eavesdropping, which also makes them stay.

02 — Visual Hooks

What they see before you say anything.

The eye stops before the brain does. If the first frame doesn't earn a second look, the spoken hook never gets heard.

VIS — 01

Slide Into Frame

Setup

Static camera already rolling on an empty background. You glide in sideways from off-screen — no entrance warning. Text overlay fires as your body fully enters.

The move

Hold a small flexible tripod as you slide in. It signals intentionality — this wasn't accidental, this was set up.

Why it works

The unexpected lateral movement registers as an event. Viewer stops to figure out what just happened.

VIS — 02

Pick Up The Camera

Setup

Camera rests face-up on a surface. Shot starts on the ceiling. Your hand drops into frame, grabs the camera, and tilts it upward to reveal your face mid-motion.

The move

As the camera reaches eye level, stare directly into the lens. Don't smile yet. Let the tension sit for half a second before your text fires.

Why it works

The camera becoming the subject — then flipping to reveal you — forces the brain to recalibrate. That recalibration is watch time.

VIS — 03

Camera Interruption

Setup

Shot opens completely black — covered by your palm. You pull your hand away to reveal yourself already mid-expression, mid-gesture.

The move

When the hand lifts, be already reacting — eyebrow raised, body leaning slightly in, hand gesturing. You're mid-sentence, not starting one.

Why it works

A black reveal feels like a cut, not a start. The viewer thinks they missed something and stays to catch up.

VIS — 04

Setting The Camera Down

Setup

You're holding the camera close — tight on your face. You step forward, set it down on a surface deliberately, step back. The full environment reveals itself.

The move

After stepping back, adjust your jacket or posture — as if you're preparing to say something worth hearing. Text fires right as you settle.

Why it works

Deliberate setup signals: what I'm about to say required preparation. That framing alone buys you 5 more seconds.

VIS — 05

Entering The Frame

Setup

Frame starts completely empty — just the environment. You walk in calmly from the right and sit down, as if you didn't know the camera was rolling.

The move

Once seated, bring your hands together in front of you. Don't rush into the hook. Let the stillness sit for one beat — then speak.

Why it works

The empty frame creates anticipation. The viewer is already leaning in before you appear. You start with the room's attention, not fighting for it.

VIS — 06

Low Level Shot

Setup

Camera on the floor, framed under furniture legs — wide shot with the furniture's underside in the foreground. You're lying flat, staring directly into the lens.

The move

Hold a small tripod setup while on your stomach. The framing should make the viewer tilt their head slightly — that confusion is the hook doing its job.

Why it works

Unconventional angles break the visual pattern that every talking-head video creates. If it looks different, it gets watched.

03 — Audio Hooks

What they hear — the layer most creators skip.

Sound is subconscious. Viewers don't notice good audio — they feel it. These are the specific sound moves behind high-retention reels.

A — 01

Sound: Dead Silence

The Hard Stop Drop

How to use

Cut to complete silence for 0.5–1 full second right before your most important line. No fade — instant cut to nothing.

Example

Music running at full energy. Instant silence. Then: "Your content is costing you clients every single day." Music returns.

Why it works

Silence is the loudest sound on a scroll feed. One second of nothing stops thumbs better than any bass drop.

A — 02

Sound: Riser / Ascending Tone

The Riser + Reveal

How to use

A building, ascending white-noise or tonal riser for 2–3 seconds — peaks exactly as your face reveals or your first word drops.

Example

Black screen or blurred shot. Riser builds for 2 seconds. Hard cut to your face. First word spoken immediately.

Why it works

Rising sound is physiologically attention-grabbing. The brain is wired to stay alert to rising tones — it signals something is about to happen.

A — 03

Sound: Whoosh SFX

The Whoosh Transition Cut

How to use

A fast whoosh sound effect timed precisely to every scene cut in the first 5–7 seconds. One per cut.

Example

Whoosh on the cut from title card to your face. Whoosh again on the B-roll cut. Keeps the pace kinetic even with no voiceover.

Why it works

Whoosh sounds register as motion even when the cut is static. Standard cuts feel cinematic. Viewer's brain reads it as fast-paced and worth staying in.

A — 04

Sound: Sparkle / Chime / Magic Reveal

The Magic Reveal Chime

How to use

A soft sparkle, shimmer, or chime timed to the exact moment key text or a key visual appears on screen.

Example

You deliver your hook line. Pause. Chime plays as the text overlay fades in: "The Care Currency System." Silence. Continue.

Why it works

The chime trains the eye to read the text. It's an auditory cue that something worth seeing just appeared. Viewer doesn't skip because their attention was directed.

A — 05

Sound: Dark Sting / Low Drone

The Negative Tension Sting

How to use

A single dark, dissonant low-frequency sting or drone hit timed to a pain-point statement. One hit. Then silence.

Example

Music running. You say: "Your content is getting views but not making money." Low sting hits. Beat of silence. Music returns. Continue.

Why it works

Negative audio cues make pain feel real. Viewer doesn't hear the sting consciously — they just feel the weight of the line. That weight keeps them watching.

A — 06

Sound: Crowd Murmur / Reaction Audio

The Social Proof Ambience

How to use

Layer faint crowd noise, ambient reaction audio, or social validation sounds under your opening line or B-roll — not the music, underneath it.

Example

You walk into frame. Faint crowd murmur underneath. Text: "12 million people watched this post." Crowd fades. Silence. Your hook drops.

Why it works

Social proof isn't just visual. Hearing a crowd response before you've said anything positions you as someone already known — not trying to be.

04 — Text Hooks

The overlay running parallel to everything else.

Text hooks aren't captions on your video. They're a second hook — running at the same time as your verbal, creating tension the viewer has to resolve.

T — 01

The Contradiction

Framework

Line 1: [Widely accepted belief]. Line 2: [Hard contradiction]. No explanation yet — let it sit.

Example

"More content = more growth." / "Wrong. Here's what actually does."

Why it works

Two-line format creates a visual tension break. Reader can't scroll past unresolved contradiction.

T — 02

The Specific Data Claim

Framework

[Exact number] [specific people/accounts] are making [specific mistake] right now.

Example

"9 out of 10 business owners posting daily are doing it wrong. Here's which one you are."

Why it works

Data in a text overlay reads as evidence, not opinion. Even skeptical viewers stay to find out if they're the exception.

T — 03

The Identity Challenge

Framework

You think you're a [label]. You're actually a [reframe].

Example

"You think you're a content creator. You're actually a salesperson with bad packaging."

Why it works

Identity challenges are impossible to scroll past. Every viewer checks whether it applies to them — that's 3 extra seconds minimum.

T — 04

The Countdown List Tease

Framework

[Number] things that are silently [negative outcome] your [result]. #[number] is the one nobody talks about.

Example

"5 things quietly killing your conversion rate. #3 is the one nobody talks about."

Why it works

The buried promise forces them to watch through the list. Retention spike mid-video when you finally reach the one that matters.

T — 05

The "If You Are" Filter

Framework

If you're [hyper-specific situation] and you're still doing [common mistake] — this is for you.

Example

"If you're making $10K a month offline and your Instagram still looks like a hobby — this is for you."

Why it works

Hyper-specific "if" statements feel personally written. Your ICP thinks you read their journal. Everyone else watches out of curiosity.

T — 06

The Timeframe Ultimatum

Framework

In [short timeframe], your [thing] is either working for you or working against you. Here's how to tell.

Example

"In the next 30 seconds, your Instagram page is either attracting your next client or repelling them. Here's how to tell."

Why it works

The clock creates stakes. Urgency in the text overlay makes swiping away feel like a loss the viewer actively chooses.

Final Rule

Your hook is a promise.

Whatever you tease in the first 5 seconds — the video has to deliver on it. A great hook attached to weak content trains your audience not to trust you. They stop once. They don't come back.

A great hook attached to real value trains them to stop every single time your face appears. That's how you build an audience that buys. Not by posting more. By making the first 5 seconds earn the next 55.
